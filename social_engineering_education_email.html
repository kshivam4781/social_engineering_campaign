Social engineering attacks are common cybersecurity threats that rely on human psychology. This email will help you recognize and avoid these threats.

Types of Social Engineering Attacks

Phishing
Fake emails or messages pretending to be from legitimate organizations (banks, Amazon, IRS, etc.).
Goal: Steal passwords, credit cards, money, or infect your system.
Spear Phishing
Targeted phishing aimed at specific individuals (CEO, accountant, etc.). Much more convincing.
Goal: Gain access to sensitive information or systems.
Whaling
Spear phishing focused on executives (CEOs, CFOs).
Goal: Often used for wire-transfer fraud.
Smishing
Phishing done via SMS / phone text.
Goal: Trick you into clicking malicious links or providing personal information.
Vishing
Phishing done via voice call (fake IRS, bank, tech support).
Goal: Obtain sensitive information or remote access through manipulation.
Pretexting
Creating a fake story (pretext) to trick someone into giving data. Example: "This is IT support, I need your login to fix your account."
Goal: Extract confidential information or gain unauthorized access.
Remember: Legitimate organizations will NEVER ask for your password, PIN, or sensitive information via email, text, or phone.

What to Look Out For

Red Flags & Warning Signs

  • Urgent or threatening language creating panic or false urgency
  • Suspicious sender addresses with slight misspellings
  • Generic greetings instead of your name
  • Requests for sensitive information like passwords, PINs, or credit card details
  • Unexpected attachments or links from unknown senders
  • Poor grammar and spelling errors
  • Mismatched URLs when hovering over links
  • Pressure to act immediately without time to verify
  • Unusual requests from "colleagues" asking for money transfers or sensitive data

What's at Risk

  • Financial Loss: Unauthorized transactions, wire transfers, or fraudulent purchases
  • Identity Theft: Stolen personal information used to commit fraud
  • Data Breach: Unauthorized access to sensitive company or customer data
  • Malware Infection: Malicious software compromising your device or company network
  • Account Compromise: Hackers gaining access to email, bank accounts, or work systems
  • Reputation Damage: Compromised account used to send malicious emails to contacts
  • Business Email Compromise: Attackers using your email to request payments from colleagues
  • Ransomware: Files encrypted with demands for payment
  • Operational Disruption: System downtime and loss of productivity

How to Protect Yourself

  • Verify the sender: Check email addresses and phone numbers for misspellings or suspicious domains
  • Don't click suspicious links: Hover over links to see the actual URL before clicking
  • Be skeptical of urgency: Take time to verify before acting
  • Verify phone calls: Hang up and call back using an official number
  • Never share credentials: No legitimate organization will ask for passwords via email, text, or phone
  • Report suspicious activity: Contact IT security immediately if you receive a suspicious message
  • Use multi-factor authentication: Enable MFA on all accounts
